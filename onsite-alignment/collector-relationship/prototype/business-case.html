Strategy · collector experience

Turn a declining transaction business into a collector relationship.

Four web experiences, built and validated on real anonymized collector data, that re-engage a dormant base of 388,000 customers and convert one-time buyers into recurring collectors.

The one-paragraph version

LUMAS revenue fell from €31.0M (2022) to €22.9M (2025) because the funnel broke, not monetization. 85% of 388k customers are 3+ years dormant (~€842M of lifetime value sitting idle) and 70% bought once and never returned. The answer isn't more ad spend; it's re-engaging the base we already have and turning transactions into relationships. We prototyped four experiences that are really one collector relationship on a shared trust layer. Indicative upside: €1.0–1.5M in year one, €3–9M at scale, starting with two low-cost, fast pilots.

Revenue only held flat because higher prices offset falling volume. Acquisition has roughly halved. And the fear of spending €1,000+ on art seen only on a screen both suppresses sales and drives €1.4M a year in returns. The base is the asset we are under-using.

The insight

Stop selling objects once. Build a relationship that compounds.

The four concepts are not four products — they are one collector relationship at escalating depths, all resting on a trust layer that makes every purchase feel safe. A dormant buyer is re-opened, settles into membership as their home, and the deepest collectors reach a commission.

Why LUMAS can win this

The moat is structural and hard to copy: only LUMAS holds the provenance data for its own editions, an exclusive roster of artists, a 388k-customer base, and omnichannel galleries (New York, Zurich, Miami, Berlin). Pure-play marketplaces have the buyers but not the artists or provenance; traditional galleries have the artists but not the data.

What could go wrong
  • Artist contracts. Commission and membership first-look depend on rights and resale terms — verify with 2–3 top artists before building.
  • Cannibalization. Membership credit must drive net-new spend, not discount existing sales — pilot on high-value collectors where the math is cleanest.
  • The reactivatable online base is smaller than headline "whales". The biggest accounts skew B2B / in-gallery; genuine online private collectors are more modest — sizing reflects this.
  • Execution capacity. The concierge and commission desks are deliberately human-led and low-volume; scale comes from membership, not headcount.
