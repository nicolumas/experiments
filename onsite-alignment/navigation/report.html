LUMAS · lumas.de · Navigation strategy report

The right menu for the wrong audience

A UX, information-architecture and CRO assessment of the lumas.de header navigation — read against 90 days of click behaviour, web revenue, and the live site on desktop and mobile. It challenges the current structure rather than assuming it is correct.

Scope: lumas.de header / mega-menu navigation · Window: 23 Apr – 21 Jul 2026 (90 days)
Evidence: GA4 navigation_click (107,750 clicks, 89,620 labelled) · DWH web order value (€2.90M, 4,510 orders) · GA4 device split · live desktop + mobile navigation walkthrough
Two integrity limits kept in view throughout: nav clicks can't be tied to specific orders (share-of-attention vs share-of-revenue, not attribution); ~480 artist-tile clicks log a blank label, so per-artist attention is undercounted.

The reframe

Two facts govern this redesign — and neither is visible in the click table.

Before ranking items or renaming labels, two structural truths reset what the nav is actually for. Ignore them and you will optimise a surface that most of your customers barely use, on the device most of them aren't on.

Truth 1 · A minority surface

Only ~1 in 9 users ever touches the nav.

Nav reach is 11.3% of users and 16.6% of sessions. The overwhelming majority discover product through search, PLP filters, editorial links, ads and direct URLs — not the mega-menu. The nav is worth fixing, but its ceiling is capped: it is a steering wheel for a minority, not the engine of discovery.

45,308 of 399,680 users · 16.6% clicks/session
Truth 2 · A desktop habit on a mobile crowd

You run two different navigations — and the rich one is on the small screen's audience.

67% of sessions are mobile, yet mobile drives under half of nav clicks; desktop is 29% of sessions but 47% of clicks. Per session, desktop users open the nav 2.2× as often. The elaborate technique/theme/size mega-menu is a desktop-minority behaviour; mobile gets a wholly different search-led drawer.

Mobile 0.12 nav-clicks/session · Desktop 0.26

The artist paradox — right names, wrong click proportions

The 19 nav-featured artists drive ~60% of web revenue (€1.75M) — the curation is sound. But attention is badly misallocated across them. Gerhard Richter takes 198 clicks (a third of all nav-artist attention) for €108k in revenue; Beatrice Hug, the single highest earner at €214k, gets 10 clicks. Uhlig (€158k / 8 clicks), Guachinarte (€130k / 4), Colonna (€120k / 9) are all near-invisible. Meanwhile seven genuine earners are absent from the nav entirely: Pedro Blas €76k, Damien Hirst €66k, Ai Weiwei €58k, Stefanie Schneider €57k, Katharina Grosse €50k, Rafael Neff €46k, Robert Jahns €40k.

Read Richter as a marquee hook, not a workhorse — famous-name clicks that don't convert in proportion — and rebuild the artist grid around who actually sells. Caveat: 480 of 569 tile clicks are blank-labelled and several artist counts are single digits, so these verdicts are directional. Fixing the tagging is prerequisite to acting on them.

PART 02

UX & information-architecture review

The current nav evaluated against established heuristics. Each card names the principle, the specific violation observed on lumas.de, and the evidence.

Hick's Law Choice overload

Kunstwerke exposes 35+ links across six sub-groups on a single hover; Inspiration ~25. Decision time rises with option count. The menu is a catalogue dump, not a decision aid.

Evidence: 6 sub-groups in Kunstwerke; 15 collections in one Inspiration column.

Consistency & standards Two IAs

Desktop is a technique/theme/size taxonomy; mobile is a search-led drawer (Meist gesucht / Beliebte Seiten / Beliebte Kategorien). The same brand teaches two different mental models by device — and 67% of sessions only ever see the mobile one.

Evidence: live walkthrough, both viewports; mobile hides the taxonomy behind curated shortcuts.

Redundancy & competing items Cannibalisation

"Top 20" (top-level) and "Top 20 Künstler" (in Kunstwerke) share one URL. "Multi-Phase" appears in two menus. "New York" is both a motif collection and a gallery. Duplicate doors split clicks and blur analytics.

Evidence: both resolve to /alle-kuenstler/; Multi-Phase in Kunstwerke + Inspiration.

Match to the real world System vs buyer language

The prime browse axis is technique (Fotografie/Malerei) — a gallery's internal taxonomy. LUMAS's core buyer is closer to a decorator, who thinks in motif, size, room and budget. The menu leads with the seller's model, not the buyer's.

Evidence: "Nach Kategorie/Technik" ranks above "Nach Thema" and "Nach Preis" in menu order.

Label clarity Jargon & broken promises

"Top 20" shows 14 tiles and links to all artists. "Wert steigt", "Multi-Phase", "Cut-Outs", "String Art", "Petites/Darlings" are brand- or technique-coded and opaque to a first-time visitor.

Evidence: label ≠ destination ≠ count for "Top 20".

Progressive disclosure Everything at once

Desktop mega-menus reveal the entire taxonomy on hover with no staging. Ironically the mobile drawer does this better — it leads with a curated handful and defers the full tree.

Evidence: desktop shows all sub-groups simultaneously; mobile stages via "popular" shortcuts.

Visual hierarchy & scanability Intent buried

The highest-intent, highest-clicked links (SALE, Neuheiten, Bestseller, Editionen) live inside Kunstwerke, below the fold of a hover. The things people most want are the hardest to reach.

Evidence: Highlights cluster = 27,696 clicks, all one level down.

SEO & URL hygiene Mixed

Technique/motif use clean, indexable paths (/technik/fotografie/, /natur/wasser/) — good. Size/format use non-semantic search queries (/search/?width=from0-to550…) that can't rank or be linked as landing pages.

Evidence: live hrefs on size/format items are raw filter queries.

Fitts's Law & the mobile target Hidden depth

On the majority device, the entire category structure sits behind a hamburger, below a search field and three shortcut rows. The catalogue's depth is far from the thumb's easiest reach.

Evidence: mobile taxonomy requires opening the drawer and scrolling past shortcuts.

Aesthetic & minimalist design Signal-to-noise

Dead entries (four Artfinder tools, empty artist tiles, a price filter with zero clicks) stay in the nav, competing visually with the links that matter. Every dead option dilutes the live ones.

Evidence: Art Cloud 2 clicks, price tiers 0 labelled clicks, tiles 569.

Where Artfinder goes

Artfinder leaves the top-level browse row and becomes a persistent contextual CTA — homepage hero (as today), a pinned entry inside Kunstwerke, and a shortcut in the mobile drawer. Its four sub-tools (Raum hochladen, Kunstwerk hochladen, Kunst liken, Art Cloud) move inside the tool. Rationale: the data shows people launch it from a CTA (1,970 starts) and ignore it as a menu (four tools under 40 clicks each). This preserves the strategic bet while reclaiming a top-level slot — dropping the top level from six items to five.

PART 06

Risks & validation

For each significant move: the risk, the assumption underneath it, how to validate before rolling out, and the metric that proves it worked.

MoveRiskAssumptionValidation & success metric
Merge / rebuild the artist grid Losing SEO equity & brand real estate for artist discovery. Tile clicks are genuinely low — but 480 are blank-labelled. Fix tagging first, observe 2–4 weeks, then A/B the merged structure. Metric: nav→artist-PDP CTR, artist-page entrances, artist-attributed revenue.
Promote SALE / merchandising higher Cannibalising full-price browse; AOV erosion. High-intent shoppers already seek these shortcuts. A/B promoted vs nested. Metric: conversion rate and AOV together — guard the 83.5%-of-revenue Limited-Edition mix so it doesn't fall.
Mobile-first drawer redesign Disrupting the 67% majority mid-funnel. Search-led + shortcut model beats a buried taxonomy on mobile. Heavy A/B + Clarity session replays + click/tap maps on the drawer. Metric: mobile PDP-reach, add-to-cart, and the mobile conversion gap.
Re-cut taxonomy by mental model SEO loss on ranking /technik/ & /natur/ URLs. Buyer language (motif/size/price) outperforms technique-first. Tree-testing / card sort before build, then A/B; preserve URLs with 301s. Metric: task-success in tree test → nav CTR + browse-to-PDP.
Demote Artfinder from top level Undercutting a strategic initiative. It works better as a CTA than as a browse category. A/B nav-slot vs no-slot with the hero CTA retained. Metric: Artfinder starts and Artfinder→PDP→purchase (must hold or rise).
Curate Inspiration long tail Hiding a collection that quietly drives niche revenue. Sub-300-click collections add fatigue > value in the menu. Move to an "Alle Collections" hub (not delete); monitor hub entrances & collection revenue for 4 weeks before pruning further.

Measurement integrity — read before acting

  • No click-to-purchase attribution. GA4 can't tie a nav click to an order; this compares share-of-attention with share-of-revenue. Directionally sound, not causal.
  • ~480 blank-labelled tile clicks. Per-artist attention is undercounted; single-digit artist counts are fragile. Fix tagging before acting on any per-artist verdict.
  • Technique & theme revenue is unmeasurable today. Keep/cut decisions on Fotografie vs Malerei or Abstrakt vs Wasser require a technique/motif tag on order lines — the top long-term fix.
  • Web channel only. Revenue here is €2.90M of lumas.de web orders; a large share of LUMAS revenue is offline/gallery and outside this surface.
