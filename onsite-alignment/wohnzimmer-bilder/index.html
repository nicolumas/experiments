Why the living room is different

The living room is the one space everyone sees — and the one wall you look at most. The right piece doesn't just fill it; it sets the temperature of the whole room.

Most rooms have a job. The kitchen feeds you; the bedroom rests you. The living room has to do everything — host, relax, impress, disappear into the background on a quiet Sunday. That makes it the hardest room to get right and the most rewarding when you do.

Art is what tips a living room from furnished to considered. A single well-chosen piece can anchor a sofa, balance a tall window, or carry an entire wall that paint and shelving never could. The trick is matching the work not just to your taste, but to the scale, light and rhythm of the room itself.

Below, we've broken it down the way we'd advise a collector in one of our galleries: first the approach, then the practical rules, then the pieces themselves.

Choose your approach

Before you choose a piece, choose an approach. Four work for almost every living room.

01

The Statement Piece

One large work, given room to breathe. The cleanest, most confident choice — and the most forgiving, because there's nothing to balance. Aim for roughly two-thirds the width of the furniture beneath it.

Best for: high ceilings, minimalist interiors, a wall you want to define in one move.

02

The Gallery Wall

A curated cluster of smaller works that reads as one composition. More personal, more flexible — and more demanding. Keep consistent spacing (5–8 cm) and let one piece lead.

Best for: collectors, characterful rooms, walls that flank a TV or run along a sofa.

See gallery wall sets →
03

The Pair or Triptych

Two or three works in dialogue. A diptych frames a sofa with symmetry; a triptych carries a long wall without the busyness of a full gallery hang. The most architectural of the four.

Best for: wide walls, symmetrical rooms, above long sideboards.

Browse diptychs & triptychs →
04

The Leaning Edit

Unhung and intentional — pieces leaned on a console, shelf or floor, often layered. Reads as relaxed and gallery-like, and lets you rotate works without touching the wall.

Best for: renters, evolving collections, softer, eclectic interiors.

How to hang art in a living room

The four rules that solve 90% of living-room hanging questions.

1

Eye level is 145–150 cm to the centre.

The standard gallery height. Measure to the centre of the work, not the top. In a living room where you're mostly seated, you can drop slightly lower.

2

Above a sofa, hang 15–25 cm above the back.

Close enough to relate to the furniture, high enough to clear heads. The art should feel connected to the sofa, not floating above it.

3

Match width to the furniture.

A piece or arrangement should span roughly two-thirds to three-quarters of the sofa or console beneath it. Too small is the single most common mistake — and the easiest to avoid.

4

Leave breathing room.

Don't crowd the ceiling, a corner or a light switch. Negative space is part of the composition.

Curator's note

When in doubt, size up. A piece that feels slightly too big almost always looks intentional; one that's too small always looks like an afterthought.

What size, where

A quick reference for the walls that come up most.

Above a 3-seat sofa (≈210 cm)One piece 100–140 cm wide, or a pair / triptych spanning ~150 cm.
Above a console or sideboardOne piece about ⅔ the console width, or a small leaning edit.
A large empty feature wallOne oversized statement piece, or a gallery wall built on a clear grid.
Narrow wall / between windowsA single vertical-format work.

FAQ

Living room art, answered

What size art should I hang in a living room?
As a rule, art above a sofa or console should span two-thirds to three-quarters of the furniture's width. For a standard three-seat sofa, that's a single piece around 100–140 cm wide, or a pair or triptych spanning a similar width.
How high should I hang art above the sofa?
Hang it 15–25 cm above the top of the sofa back, with the centre of the work around 145–150 cm from the floor. This keeps the art connected to the furniture while clearing the heads of anyone seated.
Statement piece or gallery wall — which is better?
A statement piece suits minimalist rooms and high walls and is the easiest to get right. A gallery wall suits characterful, personal spaces but needs consistent spacing and a unifying thread. Neither is "better" — it depends on your room and how hands-on you want to be.
What kind of art works best in a living room?
The art that best matches your room's scale, light and palette. Minimalist rooms benefit from one bold work; warm interiors from echoed earthy tones; bold rooms from a single confident statement. Photography and abstracts are the most versatile starting points.
