II · Conception

On masking, and the names of goddesses.

Each work in the series is named for a goddess or an icon of the Black feminine — Erzulie, Osun, Idia, Iris, bell. This one is named Aset: the Egyptian Isis, keeper of magic, healing and the throne.
In the studies, her own gloss on the work: “Amour into armour.” Ornament, revealed as protection.

Masking is among the oldest grammars of African art — a way of becoming someone, or something, other than oneself for the length of a ceremony. Muthiga-Oyekunle borrows its logic for the still image: she covers the photographed face with an assembled mask, and in doing so turns a portrait into an apparition.

The covering is built from the natural world — the fins of a lionfish, the eye of a peacock feather, sea anemones, the wings of butterflies — scanned, re-cut and laid over a black-and-white portrait on a ground of gold. What looks like adornment is closer to protection. The face is given, but only in part.

To name the work Aset is to place that face within a far older lineage of powerful women — divine, remembered, renamed across the diaspora.

Masking, Professor Chika Okeke-Agulu points out, is in decline — and "yet it makes one wonder, with great regret, what knowledge of masking in Africa is now forever lost." Chika Okeke-Agulu, quoted in the artist's monograph1

1 On the political, religious and cultural pressures bearing on traditional modes of life. "What is remarkable," he writes, "is that it has not declined more."

2 Aset / Auset — the Kemetic name for the goddess the Greeks called Isis: throne, mother, magician, protector of the dead.
